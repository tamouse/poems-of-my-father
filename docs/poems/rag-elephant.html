Poems of My Father

Rag Elephant

An admiration of a poem by Carlos Drummond de Andrade

The bones are pieces of a broken chair. The skin is heavy cotton, wool for hair. Buttons, of course, clever threads, a face. Four walking cans. Ears; floppy pillowcase.

A rolled up trunk, eager to hold, to lift. Real tusks. That lasting liquid eye, a gift. On each side, glued-on flowers and small clouds.

No one looks or smiles in all the crowds He ambles through; so, tired past love tonight, Each piece he will fold neatly, wrap just right.

For now, he strides, he sways. He owns the street. The tail almost drops off, cloth drags, his feet Scrape. The too-thin chair parts creak and bend. He is all grace; hoping to find a sad friend.

Poetry of Robert Lyle Temple, 1928-2018 (oldbob, dad, pa, uncle bob, popka, grampa bob, the professor)
