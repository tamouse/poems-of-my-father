Poems of My Father

Nineteen Sixty-eight

The comfort of the kids who took to streets, brave hearts on tattered sleeves, for bringing Prufrock from obtuse to obvious, grieves these added years; their green words failing after a rainless season in this mid-summer orchard with October leaves.

Foolish were they in the cruel but flowered April of their year. neglecting the unnamed orphan who longed for life, only to call her back now bedded but not embraced to be a barefoot wife holding in compact purposes a homemade pruning knife.

So must the young years grieve, not so much for the tattered of heart, not for the undreamed child, as for her â€“ call her Winged Victory, a dream of ancient heroines where now and then she smiled; not now beautiful, see her the waif-wife barren and wasted as if once more exiled.

Tenderness new not a recradled couple with lovesong of the mime, for no half measured serenades endear or charm, a stronger hold belongs in this harsh time: enfold both fear and faith as from opposing prime she young to you yields in your now newly ageless arms.

Poetry of Robert Lyle Temple, 1928-2018 (oldbob, dad, pa, uncle bob, popka, grampa bob, the professor)
