Poems of My Father

White Water

A Namakagon runaway canoe racked up on rocks. A boy, then twelve years old a boyscout, did no more than he was told (Kid, get that paddle?) stepped in churning blue – the speaker teasing had not thought he’d do the act – the boy was swept off thrown and rolled came home with such a story to unfold: words phrased as torrents he had tumbled through

He could not tell enough – a brownish grey and metal-flavored memory would stay to bruise – years on – a normal afternoon – seen not unlike a distant half-heard tune not recognizable but flowing still as sung by his own ghost against his will.

Poetry of Robert Lyle Temple, 1928-2018 (oldbob, dad, pa, uncle bob, popka, grampa bob, the professor)
