Poems of My Father

Three Acts

Ginette had said the most disturbing thing: “Guess what? Won’t need this window when I’m dead” “What’s this? What put that notion in your head?” “I’ll walk through windows, see through walls. They’ll bring My coffin here, I’ll hop right out. I’ll jump On to your lap and you won’t even know– I’ll snuggle close to you…” “My God, my dear, Must you go on!” I said. “Oh, you old grump…” She called me that when she felt picked on. “Oh, You grump!” We said all this… was it last year? I wash this window, wash this window, bring My chair to sit here next to hers–my head Filled with one racking question: When we’re dead, Do we need windows? …walls, or anything?

Poetry of Robert Lyle Temple, 1928-2018 (oldbob, dad, pa, uncle bob, popka, grampa bob, the professor)
